# Educational Tips

Use one or two short tips. Keep them practical and tied to the analyzed content.

## Lateral Reading

Before reading a questionable page deeply, leave it. Search who owns it, what
other reliable sources say about it, and whether its author is identifiable.

## Source Triple-Check

Check the claim, the source, and the evidence separately. A real source can make a
bad claim, and a true data point can be used in a misleading way.

## Screenshot Caution

Screenshots are easy to crop, date-shift, or fabricate. Ask for the original link,
archive, or primary source before treating a screenshot as evidence.

## Statistics

Percentages need denominators. Ask: out of how many, over what period, compared
with what baseline, and measured how?

## Health Claims

Adverse-event reporting systems collect reports for investigation. A report means
"this happened after" an event, not "this was caused by" the event. Look for
systematic reviews, regulator assessments, and clinical evidence.

## Conspiracy Framing

"They do not want you to know" is a cue to slow down. Ask what evidence would
change the author's mind. If no evidence could, the claim is not being argued
scientifically.

## Old Content Recycled as New

Reverse-search images and exact phrases. Old photos and headlines are often
reused during crises to create false urgency.

## Share-Safe Correction

Lead with respect, not ridicule:

```text
I checked this because it is important. The available evidence does not support
the claim that [claim]. The strongest sources I found say [key finding].
```

## Prebunking Pattern

When you see urgency + anonymous source + emotional language, pause before
sharing. That combination is designed to move faster than verification.
